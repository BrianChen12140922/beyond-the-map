Feng Shui · 風水

The Nine Dragons 九龍

Kowloon (九龍, "Nine Dragons") takes its name from a thirteenth-century legend tied to the boy-emperor Bing of the Southern Song, who fled south as the Mongol armies advanced. Counting the eight surrounding peaks — Tate's Cairn, Kowloon Peak, Lion Rock and others — a courtier reportedly told the young ruler that the landscape held only eight dragons, and that the emperor himself completed the ninth, since a dragon also resides in the Son of Heaven. The story converted ordinary mountains into a sacred ridge system that still defines how locals read the Kowloon skyline today.

Origin
13th c. — Southern Song
District
Kowloon (九龍)

Feng Shui · 風水

Lion Rock Spirit 獅山精神

Rising 495 m above Kowloon, Lion Rock (獅子山) is the city's most-recognised mountain — a couchant lion in feng shui terms, guarding the urban basin from the north. Its silhouette became a civic symbol during the 1970s, when the RTHK series Below the Lion Rock dramatised the resilience of Hong Kong's working-class neighbourhoods. To geomancers the rock is the "azure dragon" arm of Kowloon, balancing the white-tiger ridge to the west; to hikers it offers a 4 km ridgeline scramble with one of Asia's most photographed urban panoramas at the summit.

Origin
Symbol since the 1970s
District
Kowloon / NT border

Temples · 廟宇

Wong Tai Sin Temple 黃大仙祠

Sik Sik Yuen Wong Tai Sin Temple (嗇色園黃大仙祠), built in 1921 in Chuk Yuen Village, is Hong Kong's most-visited Daoist sanctuary. Its main hall enshrines a portrait of the Jinhua shepherd-immortal Wong Tai Sin, who promised to "grant whatever is requested" (有求必應). The temple is famous for kau cim (求籤) — devotees shake a bamboo cylinder until one numbered stick falls out, then have its accompanying poem read at one of the licensed booths along the entrance arcade. The grounds also house a celebrated nine-dragon wall and a "three religions" hall.

Origin
Temple founded 1921
District
Wong Tai Sin, Kowloon

Festivals · 節慶

Hungry Ghost Festival 盂蘭勝會

During the seventh lunar month, the Yulan Festival (盂蘭勝會) marks the period when the gates of the underworld are believed to open and ancestral and wandering spirits walk among the living. In Hong Kong's working-class districts, communities — historically led by the Chiu Chow (潮州) diaspora — erect bamboo theatres for nightly Cantonese opera, set out food on roadside altars, and burn paper money and effigies of clothing, mansions, and even smartphones. Inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list since 2011, the festival turns ordinary streets into one of the world's largest open-air rituals of remembrance.

Origin
7th lunar month — UNESCO ICH 2011
District
Citywide · Cheung Chau, Sham Shui Po

Cosmology · 宇宙觀

The Five Elements 五行

Wuxing (五行) — wood, fire, earth, metal, water — is the cosmological grammar underneath classical feng shui, traditional Chinese medicine, and even modern building design in Hong Kong. The five phases generate and constrain each other in a fixed cycle: water nourishes wood, wood feeds fire, fire creates earth (ash), earth bears metal, metal condenses water. Hong Kong's skyscrapers play this game openly — the HSBC Building's "cannons" aimed at the Bank of China's metal blades, the Lippo Centre's wood-form columns, the curved water-form roofs of the Convention Centre — making the skyline a quiet conversation between the five elements.

Origin
Pre-Han China · Warring States era
District
Citywide skyline

Daoist · 道教

The Eight Immortals 八仙

The Eight Immortals (八仙) are the most beloved figures in popular Daoism: Lü Dongbin's sword, He Xiangu's lotus, Zhongli Quan's fan, Iron-Crutch Li's gourd, Han Xiangzi's flute, Cao Guojiu's tablet, Lan Caihe's basket, Zhang Guolao's bamboo drum. Together they represent youth, age, poverty, wealth, the masculine and the feminine — a complete society of the awakened. Hong Kong's Pat Sin Leng (八仙嶺) ridge in the northern New Territories names each of its eight peaks after one of the immortals, with Hsien Ku Fung (仙姑峰), peak of the female immortal He Xiangu, as its 591 m highest point.

Origin
Ming–Qing folk Daoism
District
Northeast New Territories

Festivals · 節慶

Tin Hau — Goddess of the Sea 天后

Tin Hau (天后), "Empress of Heaven", is the patron goddess of seafarers, originally venerated in coastal Fujian as the village shamaness Lin Moniang (林默娘). Hong Kong, a city built on its harbour, has more than seventy Tin Hau temples — the oldest, at Joss House Bay near Sai Kung, was founded in the thirteenth century. On the twenty-third day of the third lunar month, communities celebrate her birthday with fa pau (花炮) processions: parades of richly decorated bamboo-and-paper towers carried through fishing villages such as Cheung Chau, Yuen Long, and Sai Kung, accompanied by lion dances and Chiu Chow opera.

Origin
10th c. — Fujian coast
District
Cheung Chau · Joss House Bay

Feng Shui · 風水

Earth Gods at the Village Edge 土地公

Tudi Gong (土地公) — sometimes called "Earth Uncle" — is the local-spirit guardian of a specific patch of land, usually a single village, lane, or hill path. His shrines are deliberately small: a stone niche or a humble red-painted altar set at a village's feng shui boundary, often where the foot of a mountain meets cultivated land. Hikers along the MacLehose Trail still pass these shrines tucked beneath banyan trees at the entrances to abandoned Hakka villages. The custom of pausing to leave a single incense stick or a piece of fruit predates organised religion in Hong Kong by centuries.

Origin
Han dynasty agricultural cult
District
Sai Kung · Tai Po villages

Timeline · 時序

Hong Kong Feng Shui Timeline

Six and a half centuries from southern migration to UNESCO listing — the long arc that turned a coastline into a sacred map.

1368 · Ming Dynasty

Feng shui practice arrives with southern migrations

The fall of the Yuan dynasty and the founding of the Ming pushes waves of Han families south into the Pearl River Delta and what is now the New Territories. They bring an established geomantic practice — orienting villages, ancestral halls, and graves to harness mountain ridges as dragon veins (龍脈). The walled villages of Ping Shan, Kam Tin, and Lung Yeuk Tau still preserve this Ming-era reading of landscape today.

1847 · Late Qing

Wong Tai Sin worship arrives from Guangdong

A Daoist devotee carries a portrait of Wong Tai Sin (黃大仙), originally a Jinhua shepherd-immortal, from Guangdong into Hong Kong. The figure — known for medical guidance and for granting requests through bamboo-stick divination (求籤) — gradually moves from private home altars to small public shrines. By the 1910s the cult has become one of the most popular folk practices in working-class Cantonese Kowloon.

1921 · Republican era

Sik Sik Yuen Wong Tai Sin Temple is founded

The Sik Sik Yuen (嗇色園) charitable order builds the first permanent Wong Tai Sin temple at Chuk Yuen Village in Kowloon, on a site chosen by feng shui masters who read its mountain backing and water flow as ideal. Today it draws several million visitors a year for fortune-telling, lunar new-year prayers, and the temple's formal classification of every wish into 100 numbered bamboo sticks.

1976 · Late colonial Hong Kong

Country Parks Ordinance creates the protected hiking landscape

The Country Parks Ordinance is enacted, giving statutory protection to roughly forty percent of Hong Kong's land area. Ridges that for centuries had been read as dragon veins — Kowloon Range, Tai Mo Shan, the Pat Sin spine — are now legally walkable, free, and signposted. The ordinance turns folk geography into public infrastructure, and quietly makes Hong Kong one of the world's most hikeable cities.

1979 · Hiking infrastructure

The MacLehose Trail opens

Named after Governor Sir Murray MacLehose, the 100 km MacLehose Trail is opened, threading ten stages from Pak Tam Chung in the east to Tuen Mun in the west. It traces the major dragon-vein ridges across Sai Kung, the New Territories, and Tai Mo Shan — letting walkers experience in two days the feng shui logic that shaped centuries of village siting.

1996 · Memorial

Pat Sin Leng wildfire — memorial at Hsien Ku Fung

On 10 February 1996, a wildfire on Pat Sin Leng (八仙嶺) traps a school group near Hsien Ku Fung (仙姑峰). Three students and two teachers die shielding the others. A memorial pavilion now stands on the ridge, and the route is permanently re-read as both a feng shui landscape — eight peaks, eight Daoist immortals — and a place of public mourning, walked annually on the anniversary.

2024 · Cultural reclamation

Country Parks 60th anniversary

Hong Kong marks six decades since the country park system was first planned. The government uses the occasion to publish new bilingual signage that, for the first time, explicitly labels the feng shui readings of major ridges — naming dragon veins and the white-tiger and azure-dragon flanks of Lion Rock — turning a tourism programme into a quiet act of cultural reclamation.
